Introduction: Repeated Purchases with Money
When you buy many identical items, multiplication is faster than repeated addition.
Key ideas
✖️
Multiplication = fast repeated addition
Instead of $1.50 + $1.50 + $1.50 + …, write: quantity × price per item = total cost.
🍎
The formula
Total Cost = Number of Items × Price per Item
💲
Dollars and cents
We can split the price into dollars part and cents part, multiply each separately, then add them together. For example: $4.25 = $4 + $0.25

Wrap-up: Checking for Reasonableness
Always estimate first — it's a powerful habit to catch mistakes!
If something costs $5.10 and you buy 4 of them, roughly how much would that be?
✅ Round $5.10 down to $5, then multiply: 4 × $5 = $20. The real answer is $20.40, which is very close to our estimate. Estimating first means you can spot if you accidentally wrote $204 or $2.04 — you'd immediately know something went wrong!
Why estimate? Three great reasons:
🎯
Catch big mistakes
If your estimate is $20 but your answer is $200, something went wrong with the decimal point.
🛒
Real-world use
At a shop, you can quickly figure out if you have enough money before getting to the counter.
⚡
Mental maths is fast
Round to the nearest dollar, multiply, and you have an answer in seconds.
✅
Builds confidence
Knowing your answer is "about right" means you can be more confident in your final answer.
